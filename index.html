  1. Affordances, visual variables, interaction flow

      Use case: A teacher who wants to deliver an academic presentation to his students.

      Zoom

      1. Affordances
        • Create meetings: Teacher can easily host meetings.
        • Accept persons: Teacher can easily accept persons in the meeting.
        • Screen sharing: The teacher can share their screens or specific applications with participants (students or other academic persons).
        • Audio and video controls: The teacher (being the host) can mute/unmute audio and enable/disable video during meetings. Any user can manage their audio and video settings during meetings.
        • Chat and reactions: The teacher can send text messages and use emoji reactions during meetings.
        • Whiteboards: Teacher can easily access a whiteboard.
      2. Visual variables
        • Menu: It is placed at the bottom of the window, being compact and suggestive.
        • Participants grid view: Displays multiple participants in a grid layout, ideal for group discussions.
        • Speaker view: Highlights the active speaker in a larger, bordered frame, focusing on the person talking.
        • Presentation mode: Prioritizes shared content, making it the primary focus during presentations.
      3. Interaction flow
        • Creating a meeting.
        • Sending the link to the others.
        • Accepting the users in the meeting.
        • Using the appropiate tools for delivering the desired presentation (Whiteboard, sticky notes options, diagrams, etc.).
        • End the meeting.

      Teams

      1. Affordances
        • Create and join meetings: Teacher can schedule and host meetings within the Teams platform.
        • Screen sharing: Presenters can share their screens, specific applications, or PowerPoint presentations.
        • Audio and video controls: Users can manage their audio and video settings during meetings.
        • Chat and reactions: Participants can send messages and use emoji reactions during meetings.
        • Integration with Office 365 applications: Seamlessly integrate with other Microsoft applications like Word, Excel, and PowerPoint.
      2. Visual variables
        • Gallery view: Displays multiple participants in a grid layout, similar to Zoom's participant grid view.
        • Large gallery view: Displays even more participants in a grid layout, suitable for larger meetings.
        • Presentation mode: Prioritizes shared content, making it the primary focus during presentations.
      3. Interaction flow
        • Creating a meeting.
        • Sending the link to the others / appointing a meeting in participants' calendar.
        • Using the appropiate tools / Microsoft tools for delivering the desired presentation.
        • End the presentation: hosts can end the meeting for all participants or leave individually.

  2. Comparing visual metaphors, idioms + suggestions for a text-only interaction

    1. User Interface:
      • Zoom: Simple and clean interface with meeting controls at the bottom and video tiles/grid view.
      • Teams: More complex interface with a sidebar for navigation, tabs for channels, and meeting controls at the top.
      • Text-only equivalent: For a text-only interaction, maintain simplicity like Zoom by having a clean interface, with basic controls for text formatting and navigation at the top or bottom.
    2. Joining a meeting:
      • Zoom: Users can join a meeting via a link or by entering a meeting ID and optional passcode.
      • Teams: Users can join a meeting via a link, calendar invite, or through the Teams app itself.
      • Text-only equivalent: Provide a simple way to join a text-based chat room via a link, ID, or directly through the platform.
    3. Reactions and emojis:
      • Zoom: Offers limited reactions (e.g., thumbs up, clapping) during a meeting.
      • Teams: Offers a wider range of reactions and emojis, including custom emojis.
      • Text-only equivalent: Implement a comprehensive set of emojis and reactions that users can easily access and use in text-based conversations.
    4. Chat:
      • Zoom: Has a chat panel that supports text, file sharing, and code snippets.
      • Teams: Provides a more advanced chat experience with threaded conversations, rich text formatting, and integration with other apps.
      • Text-only equivalent: Design a chat system that supports threaded conversations, rich text formatting, and easy file sharing.
    5. Breakout rooms (small group discussions):
      • Zoom: Allows the host to create breakout rooms and assign participants manually or automatically.
      • Teams: Requires users to pre-configure channels or meeting rooms for smaller group discussions.
      • Text-only equivalent: Offer an easy way to create sub-chat rooms or channels for smaller group discussions within the text-based platform.

  3. Cognitive overload in special conditions/contexts

    1. Zoom
      • Context: creating a meeting. In Zoom it's quite simple to create a meeting. Steps:
        • Sign in, click "New Meeting" or schedule a meeting in advance.
        • The app generates a meeting ID, a password and a link. The host can send only the link, or both the ID and password to the users in order to connect.

        Cognitive overload: very minimal.

      • Context: share screening. Steps:
        • Click "Share Screen" button at the bottom of the meeting window.
        • Choose the desired application or the entire screen to be shared.

        Cognitive overload: minimal.

    2. Teams
      • Context: creating a meeting. Steps:
        • Integrated with Office 365, sign in to Teams app.
        • Create meeting from the calendar or directly in a channel.
        • Fill out meeting details (title, attendees, date, time, etc.).
        • Set meeting options (channel, recurrence, etc.).

        Cognitive overload: complexity of meeting options, requires familiarity with Teams interface.

      • Context: share screening. Steps:
        • Click "Share" button in the meeting control bar.
        • Choose the desired application, the entire screen or the PowerPoint file.
        • Presenters can give control to other participants.

        Cognitive overload: pretty minimal, similar to Zoom, but with added option to share PowerPoint files directly.

  4. Improvements

    1. UI/UX problem identified: Cluttered UI - The user interface of both Zoom and Teams mobile can be overwhelming with too many buttons, options, and menus on the screen.
      Solutions suggested:
      • Simplify the UI by grouping similar features together.
      • Allow users to customize their interface by hiding or rearranging elements they don't use often.

    2. UI/UX problem identified: Inconsistent UX across platforms - The user experience of Zoom and Teams can differ significantly between web and mobile versions.
      Solutions suggested:
      • Ensure a consistent design across both platforms (Web + Mobile) for easy recognition.
      • Implement responsive design principles that adapt to different screen sizes.

    3. UI/UX problem identified: Limited Customization Options - Both Zoom and Teams offer limited customization options in terms of branding or personalization.
      Solutions suggested:
      • Allow organizations to customize the interface with their own logos or colors to create a more cohesive brand experience.
      • Give individual users more control over how they interact with the application by allowing them to customize settings such as notification preferences or default meeting layouts.

  5. Empiric laws

    For remote communication applications like Teams and Meets, the empiric laws of Fitts', Hick's, and Steering can be adapted to the context of multi-device interaction in the following ways:

    1. Fitts' Law: This law states that the time required to reach a target is determined by its size and distance from the starting point. In multi-device interaction scenarios, users may have multiple targets on different devices they need to interact with simultaneously. To adapt Fitts' Law in this context, designers should consider not only the size and distance of each target but also their relative importance or priority.

    2. Hick's Law: This law states that increasing the number of choices increases decision time logarithmically. In multi-device interaction scenarios, users may face more choices as they switch between devices or apps. To adapt Hick's Law in this context, designers should aim to minimize unnecessary choices or steps in device switching and provide clear cues for users when making decisions.

    3. Steering Law: This law states that movement towards a target is influenced by its size and proximity to other objects. In multi-device interaction scenarios, users may need to navigate complex interfaces with multiple targets on different devices at once. To adapt Steering Law in this context, designers should consider how visual feedback can help guide user movements across devices while minimizing errors caused by accidental touches or clicks on unintended targets.
